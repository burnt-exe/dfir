üí≠ Worksheet 5 ‚Äì Reflection Questions

Below is a professionally written sample answer set that students may use as guidance.

  1. Why is the chain of custody essential in DFIR investigations?

    The chain of custody is essential in DFIR because it ensures that every piece of digital evidence is accounted for from the moment it is seized until its final disposition. By documenting who handled the evidence, when it was transferred, and how it was stored, investigators preserve the integrity and authenticity of the data. Courts require a clear, unbroken chain of custody to confirm that the evidence has not been altered, tampered with, or accessed improperly. This documentation protects the credibility of the investigation and ensures the evidence remains legally admissible.

  2. What documentation mistakes could make digital evidence inadmissible?

    Digital evidence can be ruled inadmissible if documentation is incomplete, inaccurate, or contains inconsistencies. Common mistakes include missing dates or times, incorrect timestamps, missing signatures, undocumented transfers, or failure to record the location and conditions under which evidence was stored. Any gap in the audit trail can create doubt about whether the evidence was altered or contaminated. Because digital data is highly mutable, even small documentation errors can undermine the credibility of the entire case.

  3. Which forensic principles (legal/NIST) influenced your documentation choices?

    My documentation follows the principles outlined in NIST SP 800-61 (Computer Security Incident Handling Guide), which emphasizes evidence preservation, integrity, and proper chain-of-custody procedures. These principles require that all evidence handling be traceable, verifiable, and accurately recorded. I also applied legal standards of admissibility, such as ensuring authenticity, reliability, and compliance with established forensic procedures. The emphasis on accurate timestamps, verifiable handlers, and secure storage derives from both best-practice forensic methodology and legal requirements for handling digital evidence in court.
